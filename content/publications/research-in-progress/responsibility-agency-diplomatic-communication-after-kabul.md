## Project Overview

This research-in-progress entry refers to a manuscript in development on responsibility, agency, and institutional accountability in military and diplomatic crisis communication. The supplied manuscript is titled *Language and Institutional Accountability: Responsibility Management in U.S. Military Crisis Communication*.

The existing website title has been preserved because it foregrounds the Kabul dimension of the project. The supplied manuscript, however, is broader than Kabul alone: it compares the Salala diplomatic baseline with U.S. military accountability briefings on the Kunduz hospital strike and the Kabul drone strike.

## Research Context and Problem

The manuscript examines how institutions publicly explain harmful outcomes while balancing transparency, legitimacy, and exposure to accountability. It focuses on official communication produced after events involving civilian or military harm, where institutions face pressure to explain what happened without necessarily producing a named causal chain that could support individual sanction.

The problem addressed is the tension between answerability and enforceability. Institutions may provide public accounts, investigations, briefings, and corrective measures, but the language of those accounts can still redistribute agency, attribute causation to systems or processes, and limit individual culpability.

## Research Questions

- How do U.S. military institutions construct responsibility in official accountability briefings, and how do these strategies compare with those identified in diplomatic crisis communication?
- What responsibility-management strategies appear in military accountability briefings that are absent from the diplomatic baseline, and what do these strategies suggest about how military institutions manage the political implications of civilian harm?
- Under what conditions do official accountability briefings assign explicit individual responsibility rather than diffuse it, and what does this distribution suggest about the relationship between institutional authority and causal accountability?

## Data and Method

The manuscript uses the Agentless Causation Composite as a clause-level discourse-analytic framework. The Salala case functions as a published diplomatic baseline rather than as a recoded corpus. The military corpus consists of prepared statements and substantive Q&A responses from General Votel's April 2016 CENTCOM briefing on the Kunduz hospital strike and Lieutenant General Said's November 2021 briefing on the Kabul drone strike.

The Kunduz corpus contains approximately 2,950 coded words and the Kabul corpus approximately 3,550 coded words. The source materials are public-domain U.S. Department of Defense transcripts. Journalist questions are excluded from coding but retained as co-text for interpreting official responses.

Coding is conducted at clause level using ACC Version 2.0. Core categories include lexical vagueness, passivisation, agent deletion, collectivisation, and calibrated modality. The manuscript also tests candidate extensions: procedural displacement, temporal mitigation, and cognitive attribution.

## Current Analytical Focus

The current analytical focus is responsibility management in official accountability discourse. The manuscript examines how institutions explain harmful events through collective subjects, agent-light causation, system and process explanations, time-pressure framing, and cognitive explanations such as confirmation bias.

The Kabul component is especially important because the manuscript analyses how the 2021 Kabul drone strike briefing framed the decision-making process, the finding that civilians were killed, and the institutional explanation of error. The manuscript links this to the broader question of how causation becomes publicly knowable in military accountability communication.

## Preliminary or Developing Argument

The manuscript's developing argument is that official crisis accounts can be formally accountable while still limiting the conditions for individual enforceability. They may disclose investigations, corrective actions, and institutional responses, while representing causation through processes, systems, cognitive limitations, or collective agency rather than through named individual responsibility.

The manuscript treats procedural displacement, temporal mitigation, and cognitive attribution as candidate extensions to the ACC framework. It presents these as supported by the Kunduz and Kabul material but not yet established across a wider range of military or institutional contexts.

## Expected Contribution

The expected contribution is to political communication, critical discourse analysis, crisis communication, and accountability discourse. The project refines the Agentless Causation Composite by testing whether it travels from diplomatic communication into military accountability briefings.

It also offers a more granular account of responsibility management than strategy-level crisis communication frameworks alone can provide. Rather than asking only what institutional response strategy was used, the manuscript examines how specific grammatical and lexical choices organise causation, agency, legitimacy, and accountability.

## Limitations or Work Still Required

The manuscript identifies several limitations. It uses a single-coder design, and the planned second-coder reliability check was not completed in time to inform the analysis. The evidence base is also narrow: two U.S. military briefings from the same national institution and conflict theatre, compared against a published Salala baseline.

The Salala comparison is complicated by differences between ACC Version 1.0 and Version 2.0, as well as by genre differences between a short diplomatic readout and extended military briefing transcripts. The manuscript also analyses institutional discourse rather than audience reception, so it cannot determine how different publics interpreted the briefings.
