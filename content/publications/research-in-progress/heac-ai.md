## Project Overview

HEAC-AI is the comparative Human-AI study in the higher education academic communication sequence. The supplied manuscript is titled *Rhetorical Construction of Scholarly Authority in Human and AI-Generated Academic Writing: A Corpus-Assisted Study*. It compares how scholarly authority is constructed in human-authored higher education research writing and AI-generated academic writing.

This project is distinct from HEAC-H. HEAC-H establishes the human-authored baseline. HEAC-AI uses that baseline comparatively, examining how AI-generated texts construct authority when prompted to write in similar thematic and rhetorical domains.

## Research Context and Problem

The manuscript is situated in debates about generative AI, academic writing, English for Academic Purposes, assessment validity, authenticity, and academic integrity. It argues that AI-generated academic writing should not be assessed only through surface fluency or detection-based approaches.

The problem addressed is that AI-generated academic text may appear coherent, explicit, and well structured while constructing scholarly authority differently from expert human scholarship. If assessors treat generic clarity, explanation, and signposting as straightforward indicators of quality or authorship, they may draw unreliable conclusions about disciplinary competence and authenticity.

## Research Aim

The project aims to examine how scholarly authority is rhetorically constructed in AI-generated academic writing and to compare these patterns with those found in published higher education research. It asks how human and AI-generated academic texts differ in their use of theoretical terminology, conceptual compression, explicit explanation, reader guidance, authorial stance, interpretive framing, and reflexive positioning.

The project also considers what these differences mean for assessment, oral verification, rubric design, assessor calibration, and AI literacy in higher education.

## Data and Method

The manuscript uses a corpus-assisted qualitative design. The human corpus contains 36 extracts from 12 published articles in higher education research. These extracts represent four thematic domains: governed university; academic and student subjectivities; legitimacy, inclusion and exclusion; and competition, prestige and academic capitalism.

The AI corpus contains 36 generated extracts corresponding to the same extract positions. The outputs were generated with two large language models, ChatGPT (GPT-4) and Claude (Sonnet), using a standardised prompting protocol. The human and AI corpora were coded using an Authority_Construction framework with six dimensions: theoretical terminology, conceptual compression, explicit explanation, reader guidance, authorial stance, and interpretive framing. Each extract was also coded for reflexive positioning.

## Current Analytical Focus

The current analytical focus is the rhetorical construction of scholarly authority. The project examines whether AI-generated academic writing uses the same disciplinary signals as human-authored scholarship, or whether it constructs authority through a different balance of explanation, guidance, and interpretive explicitness.

The manuscript also connects these rhetorical differences to assessment practice. It considers why features that make AI-generated writing readable may not necessarily demonstrate the same kind of disciplinary authority that expert human scholarship constructs through compressed terminology, assumed context, and insider rhetorical conventions.

## Preliminary or Developing Argument

The manuscript's developing argument is that human and AI-generated texts engage similar conceptual domains but rely on different rhetorical strategies. In the analysed corpus, AI-generated texts score higher on explicit explanation, reader guidance, and interpretive framing. Human texts score higher on theoretical terminology and conceptual compression, though these differences are smaller.

The argument is not that one corpus is simply better than the other. Rather, the manuscript suggests that the two corpora construct scholarly authority differently. This distinction matters because assessment criteria based only on generic clarity or structure may inadvertently reward the rhetorical profile of AI-generated writing without confirming disciplinary reasoning.

## Expected Contribution

The expected contribution is to English for Academic Purposes, AI-assisted academic writing research, and assessment integrity. The project offers a corpus-assisted way of describing rhetorical differences between expert human scholarship and AI-generated academic writing.

It also provides evidence for designing oral verification and assessment practices that focus on disciplinary reasoning rather than surface textual features. The manuscript suggests that authenticity judgements should attend to whether writers can explain theoretical choices, situate arguments within disciplinary debates, and account for interpretive claims.

## Limitations or Work Still Required

The manuscript identifies several limitations. The human corpus represents published expert scholarship, not student writing, so findings should not be transferred directly to all student assessment contexts. The AI corpus was generated through a single prompting protocol and combines two models into one AI corpus, meaning model-level variation is not visible.

The coding was conducted by the principal researcher without inter-coder reliability data. Future work should test the framework with multiple coders, examine model-level and prompt-level variation, and compare student writing with both human expert and AI-generated texts.
