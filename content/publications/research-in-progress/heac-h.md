## Project Overview

HEAC-H is the human-authored baseline study in the higher education academic communication sequence. The supplied manuscript is titled *Reflexive Positioning in Higher Education Research Writing: Establishing a Human Baseline Before Generative AI*. It analyses pre-generative-AI higher education research writing in order to document recurring discursive patterns in human-authored scholarship.

This project does not study AI-generated writing directly. Its role is to establish a human baseline that can support later comparison with AI-generated academic discourse, including the separate HEAC-AI project.

## Research Context and Problem

The manuscript is situated at the intersection of higher education discourse research, critical discourse analysis, academic writing, and emerging concerns about generative AI. It notes that higher education policy, governance, identity, and institutional communication have been studied extensively, but the higher education research article itself has rarely been treated as an object of discourse analysis.

The problem addressed is that future comparisons between human and AI-generated academic writing require a documented account of how human-authored higher education scholarship constructs knowledge claims. Without such a baseline, it is difficult to identify whether AI-generated academic writing reproduces, modifies, or departs from established human scholarly patterns.

## Research Questions

- What recurring discursive patterns characterise human-authored higher education research writing published before the widespread adoption of generative AI?
- How are these patterns realised linguistically?

## Data and Method

The manuscript uses corpus-informed critical discourse analysis. The HEAC-H corpus contains 36 extracts from 12 articles published between 2020 and 2021 in three higher education journals: *Journal of Further and Higher Education*, *Teaching in Higher Education*, and *Studies in Higher Education*.

The extract, rather than the whole article, is the unit of analysis. Three extracts were selected from each article, representing conceptual explanation, analytical interpretation, and argumentative justification. The corpus was balanced across these discourse functions to reduce the risk that findings would reflect only one rhetorical mode.

The analysis uses close reading rather than computational corpus methods. It is guided by attention to epistemic depth, argument movement, hedging, specificity, and conceptual density. The study is qualitative and interpretive, with an audit trail used to support transparency.

## Current Analytical Focus

The current analytical focus is reflexive positioning in human-authored higher education research writing. The manuscript examines how authors describe dominant discourses in higher education while also signalling critical distance from the concepts and institutional logics they analyse.

The analysis identifies four themes: the governed university; academic and student subjectivities; legitimacy, inclusion and exclusion; and competition, prestige and academic capitalism. Across these themes, it traces macro-discourses including neoliberal governmentality, epistemic exclusion and legitimacy, and performative subject formation.

## Preliminary or Developing Argument

The manuscript's developing argument is that higher education researchers often write both within and against the conceptual frameworks they use. Authors draw on vocabularies of governance, decolonisation, competition, legitimacy, and institutional change, while using hedging, evaluative commentary, ironic formulation, and scare-quoting to mark critical distance.

The source presents reflexive positioning as the principal analytical contribution of the study. The claim is corpus-specific: HEAC-H documents this pattern in a selected body of pre-generative-AI higher education scholarship rather than making a universal claim about all human academic writing.

## Expected Contribution

The expected contribution is twofold. First, the project documents reflexive positioning as a recurring feature of the analysed human-authored higher education research writing. Second, it establishes a pre-generative-AI baseline for future comparison with AI-generated academic discourse.

The study is relevant to higher education research, critical discourse analysis, academic writing, and AI-mediated academic communication because it clarifies what aspects of human scholarly discourse may need to be compared when evaluating AI-generated texts.

## Limitations or Work Still Required

The manuscript identifies the interpretive nature of the analysis as a limitation. It is a single-analyst qualitative study and does not claim to provide a neutral or exhaustive account of higher education discourse. Alternative interpretations remain possible.

The corpus is modest and purposively selected. It supports close analysis of recurring patterns within the selected extracts, but it does not support broad statistical generalisation about all higher education research writing. Further work may compare this human baseline with AI-generated, student-authored, or discipline-specific corpora.
