## Research Overview

This article examines cultural representation in a locally designed ESL textbook used in Pakistan. It focuses on a selection of literary texts included in the textbook and considers how Western and Asian contexts are represented through contrasting social, economic, and religious perspectives. The study treats the textbook not only as a language-learning resource but also as a cultural text that can shape learners' views of social life, identity, power, and cultural value.

Using a critical discourse analysis approach, the article studies the literary content through field and tenor perspectives. It argues that texts which appear to represent broadly comparable social conditions may nevertheless construct sharply different worldviews. The analysis is concerned with how cultural meanings are embedded in textbook selections and how these meanings may affect learners whose own cultural background differs from some of the represented contexts.

## Research Context and Problem

The article is situated in debates about the relationship between language learning, culture, and textbook design. It notes that ESL and EFL textbooks often function as major transmitters of culture, particularly in educational settings where textbooks and teachers are the primary sources of classroom knowledge. In Pakistan, locally approved textbooks have an especially important role because they are used widely and are expected to support both language learning and cultural understanding.

The problem addressed in the article is that local publication or official approval does not necessarily ensure culturally balanced representation. Earlier research had questioned the cultural suitability of ESL materials used in Pakistan and had raised concerns about the mismatch between learners' cultural backgrounds and the cultural worlds represented in textbooks. This study responds to that concern by examining a locally designed textbook and asking whether its literary content provides an appropriate balance between local and foreign cultural perspectives.

## Research Aim

The study aims to investigate cultural representation in a locally designed ESL textbook used in Pakistan from a critical discourse analysis perspective. It examines how selected literary texts from Western and Asian contexts represent social life, economic conditions, religion, morality, and cultural values, and how these representations may support or complicate learners' engagement with language and culture.

## Data and Method

The data come from a locally designed ESL textbook edited by the official textbook board and curriculum authority and published by the Punjab Textbook Board. The textbook is used in Pakistan and contains fifteen literary texts, including abridged, simplified, and translated short stories from English, American, Urdu, Persian, and other literary traditions. The total textual data are approximately 24,000 words.

Following an earlier division of the textbook texts, the article organises the literary selections into two broad groups: foreign texts and local texts. The foreign texts are largely associated with Western contexts, while the local texts are associated with Pakistani, Persian, Iranian, and Middle Eastern contexts. The study uses critical discourse analysis, drawing particularly on Fairclough's view of representation and social events, to examine how the two groups of texts classify and represent social experience.

The analysis focuses on field and tenor. Field is used to examine what social activities, participants, and circumstances are represented in the texts, while tenor is used to consider relationships, attitudes, and social positioning. The article does not claim statistical representativeness; rather, it offers a close interpretive analysis of cultural representation in the selected textbook.

## Main Findings

The analysis finds that the textbook does not provide a balanced representation of local culture, despite being locally designed and officially published. The article argues that the textbook offers considerable exposure to foreign literary and cultural contexts while giving more limited attention to local cultural representation. This imbalance is important because learners may encounter foreign social worlds without sufficient contextual support for interpreting their values, assumptions, and differences from local experience.

One major finding concerns social fragmentation and the representation of rural and urban life. The foreign and local texts both include social and economic difficulties, yet the article shows that these conditions are framed differently. In some foreign texts, hardship may still be accompanied by mobility, agency, or individual aspiration, whereas in local texts social and economic difficulty is often represented through sharper constraints, dependency, vulnerability, or limited opportunity. The article therefore suggests that apparently similar themes may carry different cultural meanings across contexts.

The analysis also identifies contrasting representations of moral and religious life. In several local texts, religion and morality are closely embedded in everyday life, social judgment, and decision-making. In the foreign texts, religious references and moral structures are represented differently and often less centrally. The article treats these contrasts as significant because they shape the cultural worldview made available to learners through the textbook.

Across the analysis, the study argues that the textbook's literary selections can expose learners to diverse cultural contexts, but they may also produce cultural distance if learners are not supported in interpreting the social and historical meanings of those contexts. The issue is not the inclusion of foreign culture itself, but the absence of a sufficiently balanced and critically mediated representation of local and foreign cultural material.

## Contribution

The article contributes to research on ESL textbook evaluation by showing how cultural representation can be examined through critical discourse analysis rather than through surface-level content review alone. It extends discussion of textbook culture by focusing on a locally designed Pakistani textbook, demonstrating that cultural imbalance is not restricted to imported materials or foreign-authored textbooks.

Its contribution is also methodological. By analysing literary texts through field and tenor, the article illustrates how textbook evaluation can attend to social practices, power relations, identity, cultural values, and learners' schematic background. The study argues that textbook content should be evaluated not only for linguistic suitability but also for the cultural worlds it constructs and the interpretive demands it places on learners.

## Implications

The findings imply that ESL textbook design and selection in Pakistan require careful attention to cultural balance and cultural mediation. The article does not argue against exposing learners to foreign cultures; instead, it suggests that foreign cultural material should be introduced gradually and alongside meaningful representation of learners' own local culture. Such an approach would allow learners to engage with different cultural contexts without displacing the cultural knowledge and social experience they bring to the classroom.

For teachers, the article implies that literary texts in ESL textbooks should be approached critically. Teachers may need to help learners identify cultural assumptions, social values, and power relations embedded in texts, rather than treating literary selections as neutral language-learning material. For textbook developers and curriculum stakeholders, the study points to the need for culturally sensitive selection, adaptation, and evaluation of texts.

## Limitations

The study is based on one locally designed ESL textbook and on a qualitative interpretation of its literary texts. Its findings are therefore not intended as a statistical account of all ESL textbooks used in Pakistan. The analysis focuses on selected cultural themes and does not include classroom observation, learner response data, teacher interviews, or a systematic comparison with other textbooks.

The article also acknowledges that identifying the cultural setting of some literary texts requires interpretive judgment, especially where texts do not provide explicit geographical or historical markers. For this reason, the findings are best understood as an analytical account of cultural representation in the selected textbook rather than a comprehensive generalisation about textbook culture in Pakistan as a whole.
