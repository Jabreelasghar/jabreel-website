## Research Overview

This article examines metadiscourse and contrastive rhetoric in a small corpus of academic writing produced by Pakistani university students. It focuses on how writers use linguistic resources to organise texts, guide readers, express stance, and manage interaction with the audience.

The study follows Hyland's model of interactive and interactional metadiscourse resources. It combines quantitative examination with qualitative interpretation in order to identify patterns in student writing and to consider how awareness of metadiscourse may support more effective academic communication.

## Research Context and Problem

The article is situated in the context of academic writing development in Pakistan, where many students enter university with limited explicit preparation for academic discourse. The paper notes that English-medium academic writing is shaped by multiple influences, including Urdu-English contact, schooling practices, and differing expectations of rhetorical organisation.

The problem addressed is that students may struggle to structure academic texts coherently and to position themselves appropriately in relation to readers and claims. These difficulties are not only grammatical; they involve discourse-level features such as transitions, stance markers, engagement markers, and organisation of argument.

## Research Aim

The study aims to explore features of metadiscourse and contrastive rhetoric in a small corpus of Pakistani tertiary-level academic writing. It seeks to identify problem areas and to raise awareness among teachers and course developers about discourse-level needs in academic writing instruction.

## Data and Method

The data consist of a small corpus of academic writing produced by Pakistani university students studying undergraduate programmes in business or information technology. The article analyses the corpus using Hyland's interactive and interactional metadiscourse framework.

Interactive resources include devices that help organise discourse for the reader, such as transitions, frame markers, endophoric markers, evidentials, and code glosses. Interactional resources include devices that express writer stance and engagement, such as hedges, boosters, attitude markers, self-mentions, and engagement markers. The analysis combines counts of metadiscourse features with qualitative discussion of how those features function in the texts.

## Main Findings

The article finds that students' academic writing shows uneven use of metadiscourse resources. Some organisational markers appear, but their use may not consistently support coherent development of argument. The writing can therefore appear structurally weak even where individual sentences communicate basic meaning.

The analysis suggests that students need greater awareness of how metadiscourse guides readers through an academic text. Problems are not limited to vocabulary or grammar; they include the ability to signal relationships among ideas, manage transitions, and create a reader-friendly argumentative structure.

The study also indicates that contrastive rhetorical influences may shape students' writing. Patterns associated with prior educational and linguistic experience can affect how students organise ideas and present authorial stance in English academic prose.

## Contribution

The article contributes to academic writing research by applying metadiscourse analysis to Pakistani student writing. It highlights the importance of discourse-level instruction in contexts where students are expected to write academically in English but may not have received explicit training in academic rhetorical conventions.

It also contributes pedagogically by linking corpus findings with writing instruction. The study suggests that teaching metadiscourse can help students understand academic writing as an interaction with readers rather than as a sequence of isolated sentences.

## Implications

The findings imply that academic writing courses should include explicit attention to metadiscourse and rhetorical organisation. Teachers can help students recognise how writers guide readers, qualify claims, express stance, and create coherence.

For curriculum and materials development, the article suggests that writing instruction should move beyond correction of grammar and vocabulary. Students need practice in structuring arguments, using transitions appropriately, and developing awareness of reader expectations in academic discourse.

## Limitations

The study is based on a small corpus and is not intended to represent all Pakistani university students' writing. Its findings identify patterns within the selected sample rather than statistically generalisable tendencies.

The analysis focuses on written texts and does not include interviews with students, writing-process data, or longitudinal evidence of development after instruction. Further research would be needed to examine how explicit metadiscourse teaching affects student writing over time.
