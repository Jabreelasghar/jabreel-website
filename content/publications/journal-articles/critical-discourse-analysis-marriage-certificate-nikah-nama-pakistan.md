## Research Overview

This article examines Pakistan's marriage certificate, the Nikah Nama, as a legal, religious, and social document. It uses critical discourse analysis to show how the language and structure of the form may reproduce stereotypes, limit consent, and obscure rights available to the parties involved in marriage.

The article treats the Nikah Nama not as a neutral administrative form but as a discourse with material social consequences. It argues that lexical choices, layout, assumptions, and omissions can shape how bride, bridegroom, families, and officials understand rights, responsibilities, and consent.

## Research Context and Problem

The Nikah Nama is a highly significant document in Pakistani society because it records the terms of marriage and can serve as legal evidence in later disputes. Despite this importance, the article notes that the form is based on long-standing legal rules and has not been substantially updated to address many social and gender-related concerns.

The problem addressed is that the document may appear routine while embedding assumptions that restrict awareness and exercise of rights. The article argues that both parties, and particularly the bride, may be positioned within social expectations that make consent less informed and less autonomous than the legal significance of the form requires.

## Research Aim

The study aims to analyse the language of the Nikah Nama critically in order to identify how discourse, lexical choices, and form design reinforce stereotypes and affect the representation of consent, rights, and responsibilities within marriage.

## Data and Method

The data consist of the Nikah Nama form used in Pakistan. The article analyses the document through critical discourse analysis, using field, tenor, and mode as guiding dimensions. Field is used to consider the social activity and issues represented in the form; tenor examines relationships among participants; and mode considers the form's textual organisation and communicative role.

The analysis is qualitative and interpretive. It considers not only what the form asks but also what it assumes, omits, or makes difficult for participants to recognise. The article connects textual features with social norms, gender expectations, legal implications, and religiously recognised rights.

## Main Findings

The article finds that the Nikah Nama contains language and structural features that can reinforce gendered and social stereotypes. Certain questions and categories appear to assume conventional roles rather than presenting both parties as equally informed and autonomous participants in a legal agreement.

The analysis shows that the form may restrict attention to rights that are legally or religiously available but socially difficult to exercise. The document's wording and sequencing can make some options appear secondary, exceptional, or socially undesirable. This is especially significant where participants may already be under family or community pressure.

The article also finds that the form does not sufficiently support informed consent. A legal document that carries long-term consequences should make rights and conditions clear to both parties, but the current form may allow important matters to be overlooked, misunderstood, or socially suppressed.

## Contribution

The article contributes to critical discourse analysis by applying CDA to a legal-religious administrative document rather than to media, political, or classroom discourse. It shows how power can operate through forms, categories, and routine institutional language.

It also contributes to gender and legal-language discussions by highlighting how documentary design can affect the visibility of rights and consent. The study demonstrates that revising language is not merely cosmetic when documents structure important social decisions.

## Implications

The findings imply that the Nikah Nama should be reviewed and revised to make rights, choices, and conditions clearer for both parties. The article suggests that reducing ambiguous or biased language could support more informed consent and fairer participation in the marriage contract.

For legal literacy and public awareness, the study suggests that participants should be helped to understand the implications of each section before signing. For discourse analysts, it reinforces the importance of examining everyday official documents as sites of power.

## Limitations

The article analyses the language and structure of the Nikah Nama and does not include interviews with couples, registrars, legal practitioners, or religious authorities. It therefore cannot measure how all users interpret the form in practice.

The study focuses on the document as discourse. Broader legal, social, and institutional practices surrounding marriage registration may further affect consent and rights, but they are beyond the article's primary scope.
