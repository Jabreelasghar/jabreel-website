## Research Overview

This article examines how ambiguity and responsibility were managed in diplomatic discourse following the Salala border incident and the reopening of NATO supply routes through Pakistan in July 2012. It focuses on the U.S. State Department's 3 July 2012 readout and press-mediated Pakistani responses, analysing how language allowed harm to be recognised while limiting direct attribution of fault.

The study develops and applies the Agentless Causation Composite, a clause-level diagnostic that combines collectivisation, agent reduction, passivisation or deletion, calibrated modality, and adjacent vagueness. Through this framework, the article shows how official discourse can distribute responsibility, sustain cooperation, and enable face-saving in an asymmetric diplomatic crisis.

## Research Context and Problem

The article is situated in U.S.-Pakistan relations after the 26 November 2011 Salala strike, which killed 24 Pakistani soldiers and led Pakistan to close NATO supply routes for several months. The reopening of those routes required language that could acknowledge harm and satisfy political expectations without producing a direct legal or political admission of fault.

The problem addressed is that diplomatic statements often do important political work through small linguistic choices. Words such as "sorry," "regret," "mistakes," and "incident," together with inclusive pronouns and agent-light constructions, can create enough recognition for one audience while preserving deniability for another. The article therefore asks how responsibility is managed linguistically at the point where policy movement becomes possible.

## Research Aim

The study aims to analyse how ambiguity, responsibility, and face-saving were constructed in official and press-mediated discourse around the Salala crisis. It also aims to operationalise the Agentless Causation Composite as a replicable diagnostic for identifying where strategic ambiguity concentrates in diplomatic communication.

## Data and Method

The study uses a bounded, clause-level critical discourse analysis of a crisis episode in July 2012. The central text is the U.S. State Department's 3 July 2012 readout on Secretary Clinton's call with Foreign Minister Khar. Because authenticated Pakistan Foreign Office primary texts for the episode were not publicly available, Pakistan-side discourse is reconstructed through triangulated contemporaneous reporting from mainstream outlets between 3 and 9 July 2012.

The analysis codes linguistic features including vagueness, collectivisation, passivisation, agent deletion, and calibrated modality. It then examines how these features co-occur at decision points, particularly around harm recognition, apology or regret language, route reopening, and subsequent normalisation. The article uses clause identifiers, coding rules, a codebook, and supplementary materials to support transparency.

The study is single-coder and small-N. To strengthen consistency, the article reports two coding passes separated by a washout interval, written decision rules, a re-coding log for borderline clauses, and supplementary documentation. The reliability procedure is framed as a robustness check rather than an independent reliability estimate.

## Main Findings

The analysis finds that the Agentless Causation Composite clusters most strongly at the decision edge, where recognition of harm and policy movement had to be negotiated simultaneously. In the U.S. readout, clauses combining a joint subject, vague causation, agent-light phrasing, sympathy, and passive construction allowed the statement to express sorrow and acknowledge loss without direct admission of responsibility.

The study shows that collectivisation played a central role. Inclusive or elastic uses of "we" and joint references supported bilateral readings while also allowing institutional ambiguity. This pronoun elasticity helped the wording travel across audiences: it could be read as cooperative by diplomatic partners while limiting unilateral culpability.

The Pakistan-side press-mediated discourse narrowed the meaning of the U.S. language by distinguishing acknowledgment from acceptance of responsibility. This response preserved domestic sovereignty concerns while still allowing the reopening decision to proceed. The article therefore treats Pakistani uptake not as passive acceptance but as a recalibration of the same ambiguous material for local political legitimacy.

Reception patterns also matter. Early reporting amplified the connection between "sorry" and route reopening, converting the mitigation language into headline-level policy movement. By 9 July, the mitigation composite declined and forward-looking normalisation frames became more prominent. The article reads this as a threshold effect: responsibility-management language concentrates when the policy decision is still being secured and recedes once movement has occurred.

## Contribution

The article contributes to critical discourse analysis by turning a recurrent cluster of linguistic features into an operational diagnostic. Rather than treating passives, pronouns, modality, or vague lexis as separate devices, the study analyses their co-occurrence at clause and turn level as the Agentless Causation Composite.

It also contributes to research on diplomatic apologies, crisis communication, and political accountability. The article shows how strategic ambiguity can enable cooperation in a crisis while preserving space around liability. Its contribution is cautious: it does not claim that wording alone caused the reopening of supply routes, but it demonstrates how language structured the communicative conditions under which that policy movement became publicly manageable.

## Implications

The findings imply that diplomatic communication should be analysed through patterns of co-occurrence rather than isolated lexical items. For negotiators and spokespersons, the study shows how inclusive pronouns, regret language, and agent-light constructions can help manage exposure, but also how these choices may require careful clarification when sovereignty or accountability is politically sensitive.

For CDA researchers and students, the article provides a portable coding approach for examining responsibility management in other diplomatic or institutional crises. It suggests that future comparative work can test whether ACC-like patterns appear at similar decision points in other cases of military harm, apology, and institutional accountability.

## Limitations

The article identifies several limitations. It is a small-N, single-coder study focused on English-language official and press-mediated discourse from a short period in July 2012. Authenticated Pakistan Foreign Office primary texts were not publicly available, so Pakistan-side discourse is treated as press-mediated rather than as direct official transcript evidence.

The study also lacks systematic access to Urdu television talk-show transcripts from the relevant week, which means broadcast discourse is under-represented. The article notes that a two-coder reliability pass and a modest pre- and post-event time series would strengthen generalisability. Its claims are therefore bounded to the texts analysed and to the diagnostic value of ACC within this crisis episode.
