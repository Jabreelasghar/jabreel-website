## Research Overview

This article critically compares Pakistan's Single Nation Curriculum with the earlier National Curriculum for English Language. It examines the claims made about the Single Nation Curriculum as a major national reform and assesses whether the new document introduces substantive changes in policy, content, methodology, assessment, and pedagogical direction.

The article focuses particularly on English language teaching. It argues that although the Single Nation Curriculum was presented as a significant national initiative, much of its structure and content closely follows the previous 2006 curriculum. The study therefore asks whether the document represents reform or mainly repackages earlier curriculum assumptions.

## Research Context and Problem

The Single Nation Curriculum was launched as a major educational reform in Pakistan and was publicly framed as an attempt to create a more unified national approach. The article situates this initiative in a wider debate about curriculum reform, public education, and the relationship between policy claims and classroom practice.

The problem addressed is that policy documents may claim innovation without introducing meaningful changes in educational practice. If a new curriculum repeats earlier goals, content lists, and methodological assumptions, it may not address the persistent weaknesses that earlier reforms failed to resolve. The article therefore compares the two curriculum documents to assess the extent of actual change.

## Research Aim

The study aims to verify the claims made for the Single Nation Curriculum by comparing it with the National Curriculum for English Language 2006, which the newer document acknowledges as a major reference. It seeks to identify similarities and differences in curriculum framing, content, methodology, and assessment.

## Data and Method

The data consist of the Single Nation Curriculum and the earlier National Curriculum for English Language. The article conducts a critical document analysis of relevant sections, including preambles, introductions, content lists, learning objectives, assessment-related statements, and methodological guidance.

The comparison is qualitative and interpretive. It examines the wording, structure, and stated priorities of the two curriculum documents and evaluates whether the newer document establishes a clearer connection between research, policy, and classroom practice.

## Main Findings

The article finds that the Single Nation Curriculum closely imitates the earlier 2006 curriculum in several important respects. Although it is presented as a new national reform, the document does not appear to introduce substantial changes in English language teaching policy, content, or methodology.

The analysis identifies similarity in curriculum aims, content organisation, and pedagogical assumptions. The article argues that the newer document does not adequately address the practical problems that had already affected the implementation of earlier curriculum goals, including the weak connection between learning objectives, teaching methods, assessment, and classroom materials.

The study also suggests that the Single Nation Curriculum does not sufficiently establish links between research and practice. Without such links, policy change remains largely textual and may not lead to improved classroom outcomes. The article therefore questions whether the curriculum can fulfil its reform claims in its current form.

## Contribution

The article contributes to curriculum research by providing a focused comparison of two major English language curriculum documents in Pakistan. It offers evidence that curriculum reform should be evaluated through close analysis of policy content rather than through official claims alone.

It also contributes to debates about educational reform by showing the importance of connecting curriculum documents with teaching methodology, assessment design, material development, and professional practice. Reform is treated as a matter of implementation coherence, not only policy announcement.

## Implications

The findings imply that policymakers and curriculum writers should avoid presenting curricular continuity as substantive reform. New curriculum documents need to specify how changes will affect pedagogy, assessment, materials, and teacher development if they are to produce meaningful educational improvement.

For English language education, the article suggests that curriculum development should be grounded in research-informed practice and should address known classroom challenges directly. Without this connection, curriculum reform risks repeating earlier weaknesses.

## Limitations

The article is based on document analysis and does not include classroom observation, teacher interviews, or learner outcome data. It therefore assesses the curriculum as a policy text rather than measuring its implementation in schools.

Because the study focuses on English language teaching sections, its conclusions should not be read as a full evaluation of every subject area within the Single Nation Curriculum. Its strongest claims concern the relationship between the English curriculum documents under comparison.
