## Research Overview

This article proposes a practical model for teaching critical reading in ESL and EFL contexts. It addresses the question of how critical thinking skills can be taught through reading lessons and presents sample materials designed around Wallace's three-dimensional model of critical reading: linguistic, conceptual, and cultural focus.

The article is pedagogical and materials-oriented. It argues that learners need to move beyond literal comprehension toward an ability to connect language with social, cultural, and ideological meanings. The proposed activities show how texts from an existing Pakistani higher secondary English textbook can be adapted to encourage more critical classroom engagement.

## Research Context and Problem

The study is situated in the context of English language classrooms where reading instruction often remains conventional and examination-oriented. Earlier evaluation of the textbook used in Pakistani state colleges suggested that conventional treatment of the texts did not fully support curriculum goals, particularly those related to critical thinking and cultural awareness.

The problem addressed is that students may read texts passively, accepting meanings as fixed and authoritative. Such practice can restrict learners' ability to question assumptions, relate texts to their own experience, or recognise the ideological dimensions of language. The article therefore argues for a change in methodology rather than simply a change in textbook content.

## Research Aim

The article aims to show how critical reading skills can be taught in ESL and EFL classrooms through adapted reading materials. It seeks to provide action researchers, teachers, and material developers with a model for designing classroom tasks that integrate linguistic analysis, conceptual questioning, and cultural interpretation.

## Data and Method

The article is based on the design and discussion of sample teaching materials. It draws on texts from a higher secondary English textbook used in Pakistani state colleges and adapts them according to Wallace's three-dimensional model of critical reading. The materials are not presented as the results of a classroom intervention; rather, they serve as pedagogical examples for teachers and action researchers.

The approach combines insights from critical literacy, functional grammar, content-based instruction, and critical pedagogy. The proposed tasks guide learners to attend to language choices, conceptual framing, cultural assumptions, and the relationship between text and social world.

## Main Findings

The article argues that reading texts through linguistic, conceptual, and cultural dimensions can produce richer classroom engagement than conventional comprehension exercises. Linguistic focus helps learners notice how meanings are constructed through wording and textual organisation. Conceptual focus encourages them to question ideas, assumptions, and relationships within the text. Cultural focus asks them to relate textual meanings to social experience and cultural context.

The sample materials show how teachers can transform existing textbook passages into critical reading tasks without replacing the entire curriculum. By asking learners to compare perspectives, identify assumptions, and connect textual meanings with their own schematic knowledge, the activities are intended to develop more autonomous and reflective reading practices.

The article also emphasises the role of the teacher. Critical reading does not occur automatically because a text contains social or cultural themes. Teachers need to structure discussion, guide interpretation, and create classroom space for learners to question meanings rather than reproduce predetermined answers.

## Contribution

The article contributes to ESL/EFL pedagogy by translating critical-reading theory into practical material-development examples. It is particularly useful for contexts where teachers may wish to introduce critical literacy within existing textbook and examination constraints.

It also contributes to action research by offering a model that teachers can adapt, test, and refine in their own classrooms. The article links critical reading with the everyday work of lesson planning and material adaptation rather than treating it only as a theoretical position.

## Implications

The article implies that critical reading can be introduced through incremental changes in classroom methodology. Teachers do not necessarily need entirely new textbooks to encourage critical engagement; they can redesign questions, activities, and discussions around existing texts.

For materials development, the study suggests that reading tasks should include linguistic, conceptual, and cultural dimensions. Such tasks can help learners understand how language represents social realities and how texts may reproduce or challenge particular assumptions.

## Limitations

The article proposes and illustrates a pedagogical model but does not report empirical classroom testing of the materials. It therefore cannot claim measured effects on learners' critical reading ability. The examples are drawn from a specific textbook and educational context, so adaptation would be needed in other settings.

The discussion is also focused on reading materials and does not address broader institutional constraints such as assessment systems, teacher workload, or curriculum policy in detail. Its contribution is best understood as a practical proposal for action researchers and teachers rather than a completed intervention study.
