## Research Overview

This article examines how Pakistani television advertising has represented economic responsibility across two broadcast eras: the state-controlled television environment of the 1980s and the commercially expanded media environment of the 2010s–2020s. Rather than treating advertising as incidental commercial persuasion, the study reads it as a form of public text that participates in defining what counts as reasonable, prudent, and responsible economic conduct. Through a paired comparison of four commercials drawn from two product domains, toothpaste and life insurance, the analysis traces a shift in how economic risk is narrated, from an earlier emphasis on visible price and inflation to a later emphasis on planning, composure, and disciplined anticipation.

## Research Context and Problem

Advertising scholarship has long treated commercial texts as sites of cultural meaning-making rather than neutral product promotion, and a body of work on moral economy and risk governance argues that everyday economic conduct is evaluated against shared moral expectations rather than market logic alone. Much of this literature, however, derives from Anglo-American contexts in which responsibilisation is described primarily as an individualising process. Pakistani advertising research has addressed ethics, gender, and cultural representation, but diachronic analysis of how economic framing itself has changed across broadcast eras remains limited. Given Pakistan's recurring inflationary cycles and sustained middle-class economic pressure, the article identifies this as a specific and addressable gap: whether televised advertising continues to represent economic strain as a shared structural condition or increasingly translates it into narratives of private discipline and foresight.

## Research Questions

- RQ1: How is economic responsibility represented in Pakistani television advertisements from the 1980s compared with advertisements from the 2010s–2020s?
- RQ2: To what extent do contemporary advertisements shift the framing of economic risk from structural conditions, such as inflation and affordability, to individual self-management, such as planning, wellness, and disciplined consumption?
- RQ3: What implicit moral expectations about responsible conduct emerge from this shift in framing?

## Data and Method

The study adopts a qualitative, interpretive comparative design rather than a frequency-based or statistical one, on the grounds that framing and moral positioning are better captured through close textual reading than through counting procedures. The corpus was built through purposive, criterion-based selection rather than random or exhaustive sampling, using three criteria: domain continuity across both eras, discursive relevance to economic responsibility, and public accessibility of widely broadcast material. It consists of four commercials organised into two paired domains: a 1980s and a contemporary Colgate toothpaste advertisement, and a 1980s and a contemporary State Life Insurance advertisement.

Analysis proceeded through four stages: repeated viewing and detailed logging of verbal, textual, and visual content; identification of explicit and implicit economic framing; within-pair comparison of each 1980s advertisement against its contemporary counterpart; and cross-domain validation, in which a pattern was treated as analytically significant only where it appeared consistently across both product categories. The design supports what the article terms analytical generalisation, meaning transferability of interpretation to comparable cases, rather than statistical representativeness across Pakistani advertising as a whole.

## Main Findings

The analysis identifies a consistent pattern across both product domains. In the 1980s advertisements, economic conditions are named explicitly and displayed visually: the toothpaste commercial foregrounds an on-screen price and directly frames the product as overcoming inflation, while the insurance commercial frames economic protection through a father's provision for dependents, expressed in devotional and familial terms. In the contemporary advertisements, explicit reference to price, cost, or inflation is absent from both domains. Responsibility is instead represented through routine planning, composure, and relational coordination: the toothpaste advertisement links oral care to embodied vitality and daily habit rather than affordability, while the insurance advertisement situates the purchase of a policy within affectionate, everyday marital exchange rather than solemn financial precaution.

Alongside this shift, the tone of the advertisements moves from a direct, instructional register anchored in cost and protection toward a softer, ambient, relational register. Domestic framing also changes, from a single male provider bearing responsibility downward toward dependents, to a more evenly shared, coordinated planning dynamic between partners. The analysis treats the absence of explicit economic reference in the later advertisements as itself an aspect of framing, on the reasoning that removing overt cost language reduces the narrative salience of structural economic strain without denying its continued social presence.

## Contribution

The article's contribution is presented as an extension of existing accounts of responsibilisation rather than a wholesale departure from them. It supports the broader argument, found in work on risk and neoliberal self-management, that advertising discourse increasingly translates structural economic pressures into narratives of individual conduct. The analysis qualifies this account by indicating that, within the Pakistani material examined, responsibility is not represented as fully individualised but as relationally distributed within domestic and familial coordination. On this basis, the study suggests that the shift identified should be read as a culturally mediated form of responsibilisation rather than a direct transfer of Anglo-American neoliberal individualism into a different setting.

## Implications

Read alongside the surrounding discussion, the findings suggest that Pakistani television advertising does not deny continuing economic instability but relocates its narrative visibility, from explicit articulation to an implicit background assumption against which planning and composure appear normal. This repositioning may be relevant to media literacy discussions concerned with how commercial narratives translate structural economic conditions into expectations placed on private and domestic conduct. The article frames these observations as concerning advertising's participation in stabilising particular interpretations of responsible conduct, rather than as claims about advertising's causal influence on household economic behaviour or policy.

## Limitations

The corpus is small and purposively selected, comprising four commercials across two product domains, and the resulting interpretations are not intended to be generalised to Pakistani television advertising as a whole. The study does not include audience reception data or interviews with advertisers or producers, and it therefore cannot speak directly to how these advertisements were interpreted by viewers or what intentions informed their production. The paired, cross-domain design is presented as strengthening internal coherence and reducing the likelihood that the identified pattern reflects an isolated brand strategy, but the findings are offered as analytical rather than statistical generalisations, applicable by way of theoretical transferability to comparable cases rather than as representative claims about the wider advertising landscape.
