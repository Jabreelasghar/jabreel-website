## Research Overview

This article examines how the legitimacy of AI-assisted academic writing is constructed and evaluated in higher education. Rather than asking only whether generative AI may be used, the study focuses on the conditions under which AI-assisted text can still be recognised as accountable scholarly work. It treats legitimacy as a matter of professional judgment, authorship, and academic responsibility rather than as a simple binary between permitted and prohibited tool use.

The article centres on three interrelated conditions: retention of human responsibility for ideas and claims, limitation of AI to supportive functions, and disclosure of AI involvement. It argues that generative AI places pressure on established assumptions about academic authorship because such systems can produce fluent text without being accountable for its meaning, interpretation, or accuracy. The study therefore examines how academics draw boundaries between acceptable assistance and problematic substitution.

## Research Context and Problem

The study is situated in current debates about generative AI, academic writing, research integrity, and higher education governance. Much discussion of AI in academic contexts has focused on detection, misconduct, policy compliance, or the technical capabilities of tools. The article argues that these concerns are important but insufficient because they do not fully explain how academics actually judge legitimacy in everyday scholarly practice.

The problem addressed is that academic writing is not merely a technical act of producing text. It involves taking responsibility for claims, defending interpretations, engaging with evidence, and positioning oneself within disciplinary communities. When AI contributes to drafting, rephrasing, organising, or shaping text, the relationship between production and accountability becomes less straightforward. The article identifies this as a governance problem: how can responsibility and authorship remain meaningful when writing is partly mediated by automated systems?

The literature review connects this problem to work on academic literacies, authorship, research integrity, AI ethics, transparency, and disclosure. It argues that AI-assisted writing should be understood as a hybrid writing practice in which the central issue is not textual fluency but the maintenance of accountable authorship.

## Research Questions

- How do academics construct and evaluate the legitimacy of AI-assisted academic writing in relation to responsibility, limitation of AI use, and disclosure in higher education contexts?

## Data and Method

The study uses a small-scale exploratory qualitative design. Its purpose is not statistical generalisation but the identification of recurring conditions and reasoning patterns that academics use when judging whether AI-assisted writing remains legitimate. Data were collected through an online questionnaire combining Likert-scale items with open-ended questions.

Twenty-five higher education participants took part. The article describes them as individuals with postgraduate qualifications and experience in teaching, research supervision, scholarly publishing, or related academic work. Participants came from several regions, including South Asia, the Middle East, Europe, Central Asia, and Africa. The sample was selected because participants regularly make practical judgments about authorship, academic quality, and scholarly responsibility in professional contexts.

The structured questionnaire items addressed familiarity with generative AI, perceived risks, disclosure expectations, and the role of AI in writing. These responses provided a descriptive overview. The main interpretive analysis focused on the open-ended responses, where participants explained how they distinguished defensible from problematic AI use, when AI assistance became questionable, and how responsibility for AI-mediated text should be understood.

The analysis involved careful reading and iterative coding of the open-ended answers. Three recurring conditions were identified: limitation of AI's role, retention of human responsibility, and disclosure. The article treats these not as isolated themes but as practical criteria through which legitimacy is constructed and regulated in academic settings.

## Main Findings

The first major finding is that participants did not evaluate AI-assisted writing in simple yes-or-no terms. Legitimacy appeared conditional rather than binary. Many participants were familiar with generative AI, and 20 of the 25 reported using such tools in academic or professional work. This familiarity did not lead to unqualified acceptance. Instead, participants judged AI use according to how far the tool intervened in the writing process.

Participants generally accepted AI use when it remained supportive, such as drafting, rephrasing, polishing language, or organising ideas. Concern increased when AI appeared to shape core arguments, interpretations, or intellectual claims. The article therefore identifies a boundary between assistance and substitution. AI-assisted writing was viewed as more defensible when the tool helped the writer express or organise their own work, and more problematic when it displaced the writer's intellectual engagement.

The second major finding is that human responsibility emerged as the core condition of legitimacy. Participants repeatedly stated that responsibility for meaning, interpretation, claims, sources, and accuracy cannot be transferred to AI. Nineteen of the 25 participants expressed concern about intellectual dependence on AI. The issue was not merely that AI might generate text, but that reliance on AI could weaken the author's engagement with reasoning and argumentation.

The third major finding concerns disclosure. Eighteen of the 25 participants believed that AI use in academic writing should be disclosed. They did not treat disclosure only as a formal requirement; rather, they saw it as a way to make authorship and accountability visible to readers, reviewers, supervisors, and institutions. At the same time, participants differed on how much AI use required disclosure. Some favoured disclosure of any use, while others distinguished between substantial contribution and minor language support.

The study also finds that norms around AI-assisted writing remain unsettled. Participants differed in whether they saw AI use as academic dishonesty, and they varied in where they drew the line between acceptable assistance and problematic substitution. These differences indicate that legitimacy is still being negotiated within the academic community.

## Contribution

The article contributes to higher education research by shifting attention from whether AI is used to how legitimacy is constructed when AI is used. It argues that AI-assisted academic writing should not be understood only through misconduct, detection, or policy compliance. Instead, it should be examined through the professional judgments that academics make about responsibility, limitation, and disclosure.

The study also contributes to debates about authorship by suggesting that authorship is being reconfigured rather than replaced. In the presence of AI, authorship is less about producing every word without assistance and more about remaining accountable for the argument, interpretation, and validity of the text. This accountability-based account of authorship allows the article to explain why some AI assistance may be acceptable while other forms may undermine legitimacy.

Finally, the article offers a condition-based way of thinking about academic governance. Responsibility, limitation, and disclosure function as practical mechanisms for evaluating AI use. They do not operate as rigid rules, but as flexible reference points that help academic communities maintain standards under changing technological conditions.

## Implications

The findings imply that institutional responses to AI-assisted writing should move beyond simple permission, prohibition, or detection. Policies that list permitted and prohibited uses may provide structure, but they cannot capture the interpretive judgments required in supervision, peer review, authorship, and manuscript preparation. The article suggests that governance should focus on preserving the conditions that keep responsibility visible and attributable.

For supervision and teaching, the study implies that AI-assisted writing should be discussed in terms of authorship, accountability, and intellectual engagement. Students and researchers need guidance on what it means to remain responsible for claims and interpretations when using AI as a writing aid. For publication and review practices, the findings point to the importance of transparent disclosure and clear expectations about the limits of acceptable assistance.

More broadly, the article reframes AI not simply as a disruption to be controlled, but as a development that makes existing academic norms more visible. The challenge for higher education is to ensure that authorship and accountability remain intelligible in contexts where writing is increasingly supported by automated systems.

## Limitations

The study is small-scale and exploratory, based on 25 participants and an online questionnaire. Its findings are not intended to be statistically representative of all academics or higher education contexts. The sample was selected for professional relevance rather than demographic representativeness, and the results should be read as evidence of recurring evaluative patterns rather than as a comprehensive survey of academic opinion.

The study relies on self-reported questionnaire responses rather than observation of actual writing, supervision, peer review, or publication practices. It therefore examines how academics articulate judgments about legitimacy, not how they necessarily apply those judgments in every real-world setting. The article also reflects a moment when norms around AI-assisted writing are still developing, so the boundaries identified may shift as institutional guidance, disciplinary expectations, and AI tools continue to change.
