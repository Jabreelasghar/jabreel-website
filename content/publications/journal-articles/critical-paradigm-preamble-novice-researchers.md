## Research Overview

This article introduces the critical paradigm as a research orientation for novice researchers. It positions critical theory alongside positivist and constructivist paradigms, arguing that critical research offers a distinctive way of examining social realities because it is concerned not only with describing or interpreting the world but also with questioning domination, ideology, and unequal power relations.

The article is written as an accessible methodological discussion. It explains why researchers should consider the philosophical assumptions behind their work and how the critical paradigm can guide inquiry into language, society, and social institutions. Critical discourse analysis is discussed as one research tool that can operationalise this orientation.

## Research Context and Problem

The article responds to a common problem in early-stage research training: novice researchers may adopt methods without fully understanding the paradigmatic assumptions that shape them. Positivist and constructivist approaches are often more familiar, while critical theory may receive less attention despite its relevance to studies of ideology, language, and social change.

The problem addressed is therefore both methodological and educational. Without awareness of research paradigms, researchers may treat methodology as a technical procedure rather than as a set of philosophical commitments about knowledge, reality, values, and social purpose. The article argues that critical inquiry deserves clearer presentation because it can reveal dimensions of power and ideology that other approaches may leave underexamined.

## Research Aim

The article aims to explain the critical paradigm for novice researchers and to show how critical discourse analysis can be understood within that broader research orientation. It seeks to clarify the philosophical basis of critical research and its potential value for studies concerned with social inequality, ideology, and reform.

## Data and Method

The article is a conceptual and methodological paper. It does not present empirical data. Instead, it reviews and discusses major research paradigms, with particular attention to the assumptions of critical theory and the methodological role of critical discourse analysis.

The discussion draws on research-methodology literature and discourse-analysis scholarship to explain how critical research differs from positivist and constructivist approaches. It also presents guiding questions and analytical concerns that can help novice researchers understand how critical discourse analysis examines texts in relation to wider social practices.

## Main Findings

The article argues that the critical paradigm differs from positivist and constructivist approaches in its explicit concern with power, ideology, emancipation, and social transformation. Positivist research is associated with objectivity, measurement, and observable reality, while constructivist research emphasises meaning-making and social construction. Critical research, by contrast, asks how meanings, institutions, and discourses may reproduce domination or restrict human agency.

The article also finds that critical discourse analysis is well suited to this paradigm because it connects textual analysis with social context. It can examine lexical choice, grammar, textual structure, genre, intertextuality, and discourse practices while also asking how these linguistic features relate to power and ideology. The article emphasises that micro-level textual analysis should be connected to macro-level social interpretation.

Another major point is that critical inquiry is not neutral in the same way as some traditional research models claim to be. It openly recognises values and social commitments. For novice researchers, this requires reflexivity: researchers need to understand their own assumptions and the ethical implications of analysing social problems.

## Contribution

The article contributes to research-methodology education by offering a concise introduction to the critical paradigm for readers who may be more familiar with positivist and constructivist frameworks. It provides conceptual grounding for researchers who wish to investigate discourse, ideology, and social inequality.

It also contributes by locating critical discourse analysis within a broader epistemological and methodological framework. Rather than treating CDA as a technique alone, the article presents it as part of a critical research orientation concerned with exposing hidden meanings and challenging unequal social arrangements.

## Implications

The article implies that novice researchers should make their paradigmatic assumptions explicit before selecting methods. Researchers working on language, education, media, policy, or social institutions may need approaches that can account for ideology and power rather than relying only on descriptive or interpretive tools.

For research training, the article suggests that critical theory and critical discourse analysis should be introduced as legitimate and useful options in methodological education. Such inclusion can help researchers design studies that address not only what social phenomena mean but also how those meanings are shaped by power relations.

## Limitations

The article is introductory and conceptual. It does not provide an empirical demonstration of critical discourse analysis applied to a full dataset, nor does it compare research paradigms through case studies. Its purpose is to orient novice researchers rather than to resolve all philosophical debates about critical inquiry.

Because the discussion is broad, some complex issues in critical theory, epistemology, and CDA are necessarily simplified. Readers seeking to conduct empirical critical research would need to supplement the article with more detailed methodological guidance.
