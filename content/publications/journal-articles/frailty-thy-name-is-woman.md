## Research Overview

This article examines gender construction in a Pakistani higher secondary ESL textbook. It focuses on the ways male and female characters are represented in selected literary texts and asks how language, narrative roles, and participant positioning contribute to the construction of gendered power relations. The study argues that gender representation cannot be assessed only by counting the presence of male and female characters; it must also consider how those characters are positioned, empowered, restricted, or subordinated through discourse.

The article uses a critical discourse analysis perspective to show that female characters may appear visible, courageous, sincere, or intellectually capable while still being placed in secondary positions within the narrative structure. The study therefore treats textbook literature as a site where learners may encounter implicit social expectations about gender, authority, family roles, and decision-making.

## Research Context and Problem

The article is situated within research on gender representation in textbooks and curriculum. It notes that biased gender representation can affect learners' perceptions of society, gender roles, and social equality. In Pakistan, textbooks remain central classroom resources, which makes the representation of gender in officially used materials particularly significant.

Previous research on Pakistani textbooks had reported underrepresentation of women and persistent gender stereotyping across subjects and grade levels. The problem addressed in this article is that even when textbooks appear to include female characters, language use and narrative structure may still reproduce male dominance. The study therefore moves beyond simple visibility and examines how gendered authority and social positioning are constructed within literary texts taught to higher secondary learners.

## Research Aim

The study aims to analyse how gender is constructed in a textbook taught at the higher secondary level in Pakistan. It examines the representation of male and female characters, the distribution of active and passive roles, the language used to frame gendered behaviour, and the extent to which apparently strong female characters are ultimately subordinated within the texts.

## Data and Method

The data come from an ESL textbook edited by the official textbook board and curriculum authority in Pakistan and published by the Punjab Textbook Board. The textbook is taught at the higher secondary level and contains fifteen literary texts drawn from English, American, Urdu, Persian, and other literary traditions. These texts include abridged, simplified, and translated stories and are printed without illustrations.

The study divides the textbook material into foreign texts and local texts, following an earlier classification. It then analyses participant roles according to gender, including the number of male and female main actors and the presence of silent or less active individuals and groups. The article reports that there are 17 female and 35 male main actors across the fifteen texts, with male actors making up 67% of the main participants.

The analysis is conducted within a critical discourse analysis framework, with attention to language and power, framing, field, participant roles, and the processes associated with characters. Because of the size of the data, the study focuses on content-level analysis rather than detailed clause-by-clause analysis. It also acknowledges the interpretive position of the analysts and the possibility that readers from different cultural backgrounds may read particular character roles differently.

## Main Findings

The study finds that male characters dominate the textbook both numerically and discursively. Male actors are more frequent as main participants, and male pronouns and references appear more prominently in the selected texts. This pattern supports the article's argument that male identity functions as a default gender position within the textbook.

The analysis also shows that female participants are often positioned as less active, more dependent, or subject to male authority. Even where female characters appear strong, brave, intelligent, devoted, or morally admirable, their agency is frequently limited by narrative outcomes in which male characters retain final decision-making power. The article discusses examples in which female characters' apparent strength does not translate into independent authority within the family or social order.

Another finding concerns domestic and familial power relations. The texts often represent male figures as decision-makers, providers, or controlling authorities, while female figures are placed in supporting or subordinate roles. This pattern appears across both foreign and local texts, although specific cultural contexts and narrative details differ. The article connects these patterns with wider research showing that Pakistani textbooks often represent male-dominated family structures and assign women secondary positions.

The study further argues that the literary texts may be historically or culturally distant from contemporary society, but the textbook does not always provide sufficient contextual framing. As a result, learners may treat gender roles in the texts as socially representative rather than as products of particular periods, genres, or cultural settings. The article identifies this as an educational concern because the learners are at a formative stage in developing social perceptions.

## Contribution

The article contributes to textbook and curriculum research by showing that gender bias can persist even where female characters are present and apparently significant. Its analysis demonstrates the importance of examining representation at the level of narrative function, power relation, decision-making, and discourse, rather than relying only on counts of male and female characters.

The study also contributes to critical discourse analysis of ESL materials in Pakistan by linking gender construction with language, power, identity, and learner social practices. It positions textbook literature as an important site for examining how educational materials may reproduce social hierarchies and gendered expectations, even when these materials are primarily intended for language learning.

## Implications

The findings imply that textbooks should be reviewed with attention to gender sensitivity and discourse-level representation. The article suggests that learners and teachers should treat textbook texts critically, identifying how individuals and groups may be empowered or disempowered through language and narrative structure.

For curriculum designers and textbook reviewers, the study points to the need for balanced and gender-sensitive educational materials. It also suggests that older or culturally distant literary texts may require explicit contextualisation so that students do not uncritically adopt the gender assumptions embedded in them as models of contemporary social reality.

For classroom practice, the article implies that teachers can use critical reading to help students recognise stereotypes, question unequal power relations, and understand how gender roles are constructed through language. The study frames this as a matter of fair language use and critical awareness rather than as a claim that individual texts directly determine learners' beliefs.

## Limitations

The study is based on one textbook and does not claim to represent all ESL textbooks used in Pakistan. Its analysis is qualitative and interpretive, and it focuses on content-level representation rather than detailed clause-level linguistic analysis. The article also does not include classroom observation, student reception data, or interviews with textbook writers, teachers, or curriculum officials.

The authors acknowledge that analysis is shaped by the analysts' cultural positioning and schematic knowledge. Readers from different cultural backgrounds may interpret some character roles differently. The findings should therefore be understood as an analytical account of gender construction in the selected textbook rather than as a statistically generalisable claim about all textbook literature or all learner responses.
