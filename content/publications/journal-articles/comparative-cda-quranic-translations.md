## Research Overview

This article compares Maulana Maududi's and Javed Ahmad Ghamidi's interpretations of verse 2:256 of Surah Al-Baqarah. Using comparative critical discourse analysis, it examines how language choices, rhetorical strategies, ideological positions, and sociopolitical contexts shape their treatment of religious freedom, coercion, guidance, and individual agency.

## Research Focus

The study investigates how two influential Islamic scholars construct the meaning of "no compulsion in religion" through different lexical, rhetorical, and ideological choices. It considers how their historical and sociocultural contexts influence their interpretations of religious authority, personal freedom, and the relationship between faith and political order.

## Data and Method

The article applies comparative critical discourse analysis, drawing principally on Fairclough's three-dimensional framework. It examines textual features, discursive practices, and wider social practices in the two interpretations. The analysis considers lexical choice, rhetoric, cohesion, semantic framing, power relations, ideology, and sociopolitical context.

## Main Findings

- Maududi's interpretation uses more assertive and politically oriented language, reflecting his emphasis on religious doctrine and an Islamic sociopolitical order.
- Ghamidi's interpretation places greater emphasis on rationality, contextual understanding, personal autonomy, and freedom of choice.
- Both interpretations reject coercion in religion, but they differ in how they construct religious authority, individual agency, and the social implications of the verse.
- The analysis shows how translation and interpretation are shaped by historical, cultural, intellectual, and ideological contexts.

## Contribution

The article contributes to critical discourse studies, Quranic translation research, and religious discourse analysis by showing how linguistic choices shape competing understandings of authority, freedom, coercion, and religious interpretation. It also demonstrates the value of comparative CDA for examining ideological variation within Islamic scholarship.
