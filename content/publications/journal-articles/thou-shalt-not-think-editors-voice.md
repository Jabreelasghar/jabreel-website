## Research Overview

This article examines how editors' voices in a tertiary-level English textbook shape readers' interpretation of literary texts. It argues that textbook framing can do more than introduce authors or provide background information. Editorial notes and introductions may guide learners toward predetermined interpretations, censor alternative meanings, and restrict autonomous engagement with texts.

The article uses critical discourse analysis to investigate how editorial commentary participates in constructing meaning. It treats the textbook as an institutional discourse shaped by curriculum expectations, censorship guidelines, and the ideological interests of groups with authority over educational content.

## Research Context and Problem

The study is situated in debates about textbooks as instruments of socialisation and ideological reproduction. In many educational contexts, especially where textbooks are centrally approved, textbook boards and editors can influence not only what learners read but also how they are expected to interpret it.

The problem addressed is that editorial framing may appear neutral or helpful while actually limiting critical thought. If introductions, notes, and questions predetermine the moral or ideological meaning of a text, students may be discouraged from forming independent interpretations. The article therefore examines editorial voice as a significant part of textbook discourse.

## Research Aim

The study aims to analyse how editors' introductions and framing in an English textbook shape interpretation and propagate institutional or ideological agendas. It seeks to show how editorial discourse can influence learners' understanding of selected texts before they encounter the literary works themselves.

## Data and Method

The data come from a tertiary-level English textbook used in Pakistan. The analysis focuses on editors' introductions, comments, and thematic framing attached to selected texts. The article examines wording, evaluative language, omissions, and interpretive guidance in order to identify how editorial voice directs reading.

The method is qualitative critical discourse analysis. It attends to textual features at word and phrase level while also considering broader institutional and social contexts. The analysis connects editorial language with the textbook board's role in selecting, adapting, and presenting literary material.

## Main Findings

The article finds that editorial commentary can shape learners' perceptions before they read the primary text. In the examples discussed, introductions do not merely contextualise authors or works; they direct readers toward particular moral and ideological conclusions. This framing narrows the interpretive space available to learners.

The analysis also indicates that editors may censor or reframe information in ways that protect predetermined meanings. By foregrounding certain themes and suppressing others, the textbook constructs a preferred reading. The article argues that this practice discourages autonomous learning and critical response.

Another finding is that the textbook's editorial voice can misrepresent or oversimplify literary themes. Rather than opening texts for inquiry, the commentary can reduce them to approved messages. This is especially significant in a language classroom where learners may rely heavily on textbook notes and teacher explanations.

## Contribution

The article contributes to textbook discourse research by shifting attention from selected literary texts to the editorial apparatus surrounding them. It shows that introductions, notes, and framing devices are not peripheral; they are central to how textbook meaning is produced.

It also contributes to critical pedagogy by demonstrating how educational materials can restrict critical thinking even when they contain potentially rich texts. The study reinforces the need to analyse textbooks as institutional discourse.

## Implications

The findings imply that teachers should treat textbook introductions and notes critically rather than accepting them as neutral guidance. Students can be encouraged to compare editorial framing with the primary text and to ask what alternative interpretations are possible.

For textbook developers and curriculum reviewers, the article suggests that editorial material should support inquiry rather than impose interpretation. Textbooks should create space for learners to evaluate texts independently and to recognise the difference between contextual support and ideological direction.

## Limitations

The study focuses on selected material from one textbook and does not claim to represent all textbooks used in Pakistan. It does not include student response data or interviews with editors, teachers, or curriculum officials.

The article's conclusions are based on qualitative discourse analysis of textual evidence. They should be read as an interpretation of editorial framing in the selected textbook rather than as a comprehensive account of all textbook production practices.
