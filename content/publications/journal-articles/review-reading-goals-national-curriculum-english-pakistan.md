## Research Overview

This article reviews the reading and critical-thinking goals set out in Pakistan's National Curriculum for English Language for grades 11-12. It examines the curriculum's benchmarks and expected learning outcomes in relation to previous research on textbook use and classroom practice. The article argues that the curriculum sets appropriate and ambitious goals but does not provide sufficient guidance on how those goals can be achieved through teaching methodology and learning materials.

The article is a curriculum review rather than an empirical classroom study. It focuses on the gap between policy-level objectives and classroom implementation, particularly in relation to reading skills and critical pedagogy.

## Research Context and Problem

The National Curriculum for English Language was designed to provide a coherent framework for English teaching across grades. At the higher secondary level, the curriculum includes goals related to reading comprehension, critical thinking, and learner autonomy. These goals are important in a context where reading is central to academic success and where traditional teaching practices often limit independent interpretation.

The problem addressed is that curriculum documents may articulate desirable outcomes without providing practical strategies for achieving them. The article argues that if syllabi, textbooks, and classroom methodologies are not aligned with curriculum goals, students are unlikely to develop the reading and critical-thinking skills described in policy.

## Research Aim

The article aims to review the reading goals in the National Curriculum for English Language for grades 11-12 and to assess their relationship with textbook selection, classroom methodology, and the practical development of critical reading skills.

## Data and Method

The study reviews the official curriculum document, focusing on reading and thinking skills, benchmarks, and learning outcomes. The discussion is connected to an independent evaluation of a prescribed English textbook used at the intermediate level in Pakistan. This connection allows the article to consider how curriculum goals appear in relation to actual teaching materials and reported classroom practice.

The method is document review and interpretive policy analysis. The article reads the curriculum as a planning document and evaluates its practical coherence in light of textbook use, teaching methods, and critical pedagogy.

## Main Findings

The article finds that the curriculum presents an organised set of reading goals and expected outcomes. In theory, these goals recognise the importance of comprehension, interpretation, and critical thinking. However, the curriculum does not sufficiently connect these goals to suitable teaching methodologies or to the selection and design of materials.

The review identifies a gap between policy ambition and classroom feasibility. Without clear guidance on pedagogy, teachers may continue to rely on conventional practices such as reading aloud, translation, teacher explanation, and end-of-text exercises. These practices may help students reproduce information but are unlikely to develop the critical engagement described in the curriculum.

The article also argues that textbook and syllabus design should be considered part of curriculum planning rather than treated as a separate downstream matter. If materials do not support the goals set in the curriculum, those goals remain difficult to implement.

## Contribution

The article contributes to curriculum studies by showing how policy-level goals can become weakened when not supported by methodology and materials. It provides a focused review of reading goals in the Pakistani English curriculum and links those goals to practical questions of classroom implementation.

It also contributes to discussions of critical pedagogy in English language education by arguing that critical reading should be embedded in curriculum planning, materials development, and teacher guidance.

## Implications

The findings imply that curriculum documents should provide clearer links between objectives, syllabi, teaching strategies, and assessment practices. Reading goals need to be supported by concrete pedagogical guidance if they are to influence classroom learning.

For teachers and material developers, the article suggests that critical pedagogy may help bridge the gap between curriculum aspirations and classroom practice. Students should be given opportunities to question, interpret, and connect texts with their own social and cultural contexts.

## Limitations

The article is a document review and does not include new classroom observation, learner testing, or teacher interview data. It relies on analysis of the curriculum document and its relationship to previously discussed textbook evaluation.

Its conclusions are therefore strongest as an account of curriculum-policy coherence and implementation challenges, rather than as direct evidence of classroom outcomes across Pakistan.
