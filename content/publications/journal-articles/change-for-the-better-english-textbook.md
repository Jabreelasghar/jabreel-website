## Research Overview

This article evaluates the use of an English textbook taught at the higher secondary level in Pakistan. The study examines whether the textbook, as it is commonly used in an EFL context, supports the curriculum goals of developing cultural awareness, critical thinking, and meaningful textual comprehension. It treats the textbook not only as a language resource but also as a pedagogical instrument that can either encourage or restrict learners' interpretive engagement.

The article argues that the central problem is not simply the presence of literary or culturally rich texts in the textbook. Rather, the problem lies in the conventional pedagogical treatment of those texts. When classroom practice depends mainly on memorisation, predetermined meanings, and examination-oriented responses, the potential of the textbook to support critical reading and cultural understanding remains largely unrealised.

## Research Context and Problem

The study is situated in debates about English language teaching, textbook use, and critical pedagogy in Pakistan. English textbooks at the higher secondary level are expected to support linguistic development while also preparing learners to engage with social, cultural, and moral issues. The article notes that such goals require more than vocabulary explanation, grammar practice, or recall of textbook content.

The problem addressed is a gap between curriculum aims and classroom outcomes. The textbook includes texts that could support discussion of culture, social values, and critical interpretation, but the prevailing teaching model appears to limit students' engagement with those dimensions. The article therefore examines whether students and teachers are able to use the textbook in ways that produce the intended learning outcomes.

## Research Aim

The study aims to evaluate how an English textbook at the higher secondary level in Pakistan is used in an EFL context, with particular attention to learners' text comprehension, cultural awareness, and critical reading. It also aims to identify whether changes in pedagogical approach could help the same textbook better serve curriculum objectives.

## Data and Method

The study uses a questionnaire-based evaluation of textbook use. It examines students' comprehension of selected textbook content and considers the extent to which classroom practice helps learners move beyond surface understanding toward cultural and critical interpretation. The questionnaire evidence is discussed in relation to the stated goals of the curriculum and to the pedagogical assumptions underlying textbook teaching.

The analysis is interpretive and evaluative rather than experimental. It reads participants' responses as evidence of how the textbook is being mediated in classroom practice. The article also draws on critical pedagogy and critical reading scholarship to assess whether the teaching approach encourages learners to question, contextualise, and interpret texts independently.

## Main Findings

The study finds that the textbook's educational potential is not being fully realised. The analysis suggests that learners often demonstrate limited comprehension of the deeper cultural and critical dimensions of the texts. Their responses indicate that they may be able to reproduce expected information but are less able to engage with alternative interpretations, cultural contexts, or wider social meanings.

The article identifies conventional pedagogy as a major factor in this limitation. Classroom treatment of the textbook appears to emphasise fixed answers and examination preparation rather than critical engagement. As a result, texts that could encourage discussion of culture, values, and social experience are often reduced to information to be memorised.

The findings also suggest that teachers may not consistently recognise or use the critical potential of the textbook. The article does not present the textbook itself as unusable; instead, it argues that its value depends heavily on how it is taught. With a different methodological approach, the same material could support more reflective reading and stronger alignment with curriculum goals.

## Contribution

The article contributes to textbook evaluation by shifting attention from textbook content alone to the relationship between textbook, curriculum, teacher mediation, and learner response. It argues that the effectiveness of a textbook cannot be judged only by the texts it contains; it must also be evaluated through the pedagogical practices that shape how those texts are read.

The study also contributes to critical pedagogy in Pakistani ELT by showing how conventional reading practices can limit learner autonomy and critical awareness. It positions critical reading as a practical classroom concern rather than an abstract educational ideal.

## Implications

The article implies that textbook reform should be accompanied by pedagogical reform. If teachers continue to use texts primarily for memorisation and examination practice, even culturally rich material may fail to support the broader goals of English language education. Teacher development should therefore include attention to critical reading, cultural mediation, and discussion-based interpretation.

For curriculum and textbook stakeholders, the study suggests that learning outcomes should be supported by teaching guides, assessment practices, and classroom activities that encourage interpretive engagement. The article frames change as possible through methodological adjustment rather than through wholesale rejection of the existing textbook.

## Limitations

The study focuses on one textbook and on questionnaire-based evidence of textbook use. Its findings are therefore not intended to represent all English textbooks or all classroom contexts in Pakistan. The article does not include extended classroom observation, teacher interviews, or longitudinal evidence of learner development.

Because the analysis is evaluative and context-specific, its conclusions should be read as an account of how a particular textbook appears to function under particular pedagogical conditions. The article's broader relevance lies in the issues it raises for textbook mediation, critical reading, and curriculum implementation.
